Roadway Model

A roadway model defines how roadways in the real-world are modeled in our database in order to accomplish reporting and cartographic goals.   The model defines the structure, conventions, and best practices for common editing scenarios.  

Roadbed Types

Below are examples of each roadbed type in our roadway model, including descriptions and diagrams.  These examples were designed to apply to all but the most exceptional situations. 

Note: for historical context, there are several key factors that influence the definition of each type.  These include: data collection needs on Frontage Roads for PMIS purposes; data requirements for HPMS; intersection/interchange definition for MIRE; former bent on leaning towards maximizing reportable mileage; and, other historical factors that have led us to where we are now. 

Mainlanes

Single Roadbed or Artificial Centerline (KG)

The KG represents a single roadbed, or the 'artificial' centerline of divided highways. 

  • Undivided, on-system highways are represented by a single roadbed.

  • All off-system roads (i.e. county roads and city streets) are represented by a single roadbed, regardless of whether or not the road is divided.

  • Divided highways must have a median of 0.1 miles or longer.
  • Counted towards mileage certification total.

Right Roadbed (RG)

The RG carries traffic in the direction of route inventory (i.e. from lower measures to higher measures).  

  • Measures are derived from the KG.

Left Roadbed (LG)

The LG carries traffic in the opposite direction of route inventory.  

  • Measures are derived from the KG. 

Supplemental Mainlanes (SG, MG, TG, PG)

Supplemental mainlanes are separate roadbeds from regular mainlanes used to improve traffic flow and reduce congestion. They can be divided into two categories: tolled and non-tolled.

Non-Tolled Supplemental Mainlanes (SG, MG)

A prototypical supplemental mainlane is used to channelize and separate traffic entering and exiting the route from normal thru traffic on the mainlanes.

  • SG and MG roadbed types.
  • Must connect mainlane to mainlane of the same route.
    • Note: supplemental lanes that connect either to or from a frontage are considered supplemental frontage roads (see Supplemental Frontage Roads section below).
  • Must be physically divided from the mainlanes.
  • Do not use ramps or other ancillary roadbeds to connect to the mainlanes.

Tolled Supplemental Mainlanes (TG, PG)

Toll lanes, also known as express or managed lanes, are digitized as separate roadbeds for reporting and cartographic purposes.  In addition, fully tolled facilities may have toll plazas/booths, which are digitized as supplemental mainlanes.

  • TG and PG roadbed types.
  • May or may not be physically divided from non-tolled mainlanes.
  • Include tolled lanes on otherwise non-tolled facilities, or supplemental lanes (e.g. toll plazas) on fully-tolled facilities.

Frontage Roads

Frontage roads run generally parallel to mainlanes on limited-access divided highways and serve to provide access to adjacent properties and intersecting roads.

  • Should not connect directly to a mainlane. 

  • A ramp is necessary to make connections between a mainlane and a frontage road, even where the beginning and end of the frontage and ramp are ambiguous.  See the "Ramps" section below for more information.

Right Frontage (AG)

The frontage roadbed that carries traffic in the direction of route inventory, adjacent to the RG.  

  • Counted towards mileage certification total.  

  • Measures are derived from the KG.

Left Frontage (XG)

The frontage roadbed that carries traffic in the opposite direction of route inventory, adjacent to the LG.  

  • Counted towards mileage certification total.  

  • Measures are derived from the KG.

 

Supplemental Frontage Roads (BG, YG)

Supplemental frontage roads occur where multiple frontage roads travel in the same direction on the same route. 

  • Typically connect from and to the 'main' frontage road of the same route, but may also connect to ramps or connectors. 

  • If the roadbed does not provide additional access to businesses, properties, or generally to the facility's right of way, it is not a frontage road.

  • Where two frontages pass through and continue beyond an interchange, the frontage that provides greater access to intersecting roads shall serve as the 'primary' frontage road, whereas the frontage without traffic control, which acts as a bypass for the interchange, shall serve as the supplemental.   

  • Where one of the frontage roads stops at the intersection, while the other continues beyond the interchange, the continuous road shall serve as the 'primary' frontage road.

  • Measures are derived from the KG.


 

Two-Way & Divided Frontage Roads

Some frontage roads are two-way and may be divided. In this scenario, digitize the roadbed centerline through the middle of the divided frontage road. 

 

Ancillary Roadbed Types

A variety of ancillary roadbed types exist to represent road segments that facilitate connections between routes, or between roadbeds on the same route. 

Ramps and Connectors

TxDOT's roadway model utilizes a variety of ancillary roadbed types to model connections between routes and roadbeds, where other states simply use ramps for this purpose.  The additional complexity of our model is the result of a number of factors (see Background section below).  The diagram below serves to determine which roadbed type to use for various common scenarios.

Background:

At a traditional interchange where one or more divided, limited-access highway crosses another route, the roadbeds that connect the mainlanes of the participating routes at the interchange are defined as "ramps," according to FHWA.  FHWA requires all states to report functional classification and traffic volume (AADT) for all such ramps to faciliate Intechange Analysis within the Highway Economic Requirements System (HERS) model.  

However, the proliferation of frontage roads in Texas results in an overabundance of so-called ramps, because of the additional "slip ramps" that connect a route's mainlanes to its frontage roads.  This proliferation has the potential to significantly increase the burden of reporting.  As a result, an agreement was made with FHWA that Texas will not have to report this data for these types of ramps (what we refer to as "ramps" in our model).  Instead, only specific types of "ramps"  that connect the mainlanes of limited-access highways to other routes, which we refer to as "Grade Separated Connectors," are reported. 

Unfortunately, this distinction results in confusion of the terms traditionally used for these roadbed types by other states, and the way they are reported to the federal government; however, it decreases the reporting liability for Texas. 

 

 

Grade Separated Connectors (GS a.k.a "GSC")

  • See the decision tree above. 

  • Typically connect the mainlanes of a limited-access highway to the mainlanes of another route at an interchange.  May connect a mainlane of a limited-access highway to a frontage road of the other route.  However, a GSC does not connect a frontage of a limited-access highway to the mainlanes of a non-limited-access route.  Instead, use a Connector for this scenario. 

  • Digitized in the direction of traffic. 

  • May split into other GSCs (see the second diagram below).  Thus, it is possible to have a GSC connected to a GSC. 

 

 

Ramps (RP)

  • See the decision tree above.

  • Ramps may be used to connect two roadbeds of the same route when one of those roadbeds is a toll (PG/TG) THIS IS STILL UP FOR DEBATE. 

  • Connect mainlanes to frontage roads of the same route.

  • Do not provide access to adjacent property or do not have an intersecting public roads along its path. 

  • Measures for ramps run from zero to length and are digitized in the direction of traffic.

Ramps and Dead-End Frontage Roads

  • Where a frontage road "dies" into a mainlane, the frontage road becomes a ramp at the point where it is only serving to move traffic from the frontage to the mainlane.  The precise location of the change can be ambiguous.  Imagine if the frontage were to be extended, then choose a point at which the ramp breaks away from that line and the line would continue on as frontage.  Choose to minimize the length of a ramp and maximize the length of a frontage road if interpretation is necessary.

Connectors (CN)

Connectors are generally short segments of roadbed that serve to facilitate turning.  Typically, the turning movement could still be accomplished by proceding to the intersection and turning there, thus the connector only serves to facilitate turning. 

  • See the decision tree above.

  • Connect two or more separated routes. 

  • Connect the mainlane of one route to the mainlane of another where the intersection of the two routes is at grade (i.e. not grade separated).  At grade-separated interchanges, they connect various combinations of roadbed types, other than mainlane to mainlane (e.g. frontage to frontage).  

  • Do not provide access to adjacent property (otherwise it is likely a frontage road). 

  • Digitized in the direction of traffic.

 

 


 

Turnarounds (TA)

The only function of a turnaround is to provide the ability to change direction of travel on a divided route.  

  • Do not use a turnaround roadbed type between divided roadbeds when intersecting roads meet at the same location. (ADD DIAGRAM)

 

This category includes two types:

A 'crossover' provides the ability to change direction when traveling on a divided route, and is typically at-grade with the route. 

  • The direction of digitization for these two-way turnarounds is from the right roadbed to the left roadbed. 

  • Only exist when the sole purpose of the roadbed is for turning around.  Where other routes intersect, either through the intersection or "T" at the intersection, there should be no separate turnaround roadbed.

A 'texas turnaround' provides the ability to change direction at a lower grade than the highway, in 'underpasses'.     

  • Should not extend to include lengthy turn-only lanes.

  • The digitization should be 'smooth' and realistic, without adding excessive length (e.g. to include turn lanes/acceleration/deceleration lanes) or without being excessively short so that the physical gore point is cut off.

  • The direction of digitization for these one-way turnarounds should follow the flow of traffic.

 

   

Others (OT)

"Other" roadbeds include Rest Areas, Weight Stations, and Runaway Lanes. 

Rest Areas

  • Connect the "OT" from the mainlane back to the same mainlane (this is the most common configuration for all but the most rare exceptions).

  • Digitize "OT" roadbeds in the direction of travel of the roadbed that the rest area is connected to.  If the OT is two way, and also connects to a two-way KG, then digitize the OT in the same direction as the KG it's attached to.  

  • OT roadbeds' measures run 0-length.

  • The "OT" roadbed type only provides access to the rest area.

  • Digitize every roadbed that is separated by a physical barrier or gap (using the same rules as medians (see example below)

  • The 'main' roadbed of a rest area is that which is closest to the highway

    • If there is more than a single roadbed in the rest area, the roadbed closest to the mainlane should be digitized as a single continuous segment, connecting from the mainlane back to the same mainlane

      • Each additional roadbed segment should be digitized along the shortest path from the preceding segment (see example below)

      • Digitize a single, outermost lane/path through large parking areas

  • Do not use Ramps or other roadbed types to connect mainlanes to rest areas (i.e. "OT" roadbeds)

Best Practices for Digitizing

UNDER CONSTRUCTION


General Guidance

  • Make your mama proud.  Take pride in your work.  If it looks good, it is good.

  • Digitize at a minimum of 1:2,000 scale, although closer is better.

  • Curves should appear smooth and closely follow the curvature of the road.

  • Do not use the curve tools in ArcGIS Descktop for creating 'true curves.'  These are not allowed in GRID's Oracle database.

Linework Connections

  • Where ramps, turnarounds, and connectors meet roadbeds, digitization should be 'smooth' and realistic, without adding excessive length (e.g. to include turn lanes/acceleration/deceleration lanes) or without being excessively short so that the physical gore point is cut off.

    • At the point perpendicular to where the roadbed line passes the physical gore point, continue the line straight from there until it meets the intersecting roadbed (see diagram).




Gore Points

The gore point is a triangular piece of median, barrier, curb, or paint striping where two roadbeds merge or split, such as where a ramp enters or exits a highway.  There are two types of gore points: physical and painted.  The physical gore point is the tip of the triangle where a physical barrier, such as a grass median or concrete curb begins/ends.   A painted gore is the triangular paint markings where the lane and ramp edge striping converges or diverges.  

  • Digitize to the end of the painted gore (within reason).  

  • Do not extend to include turn lanes.

  • Digitize the roadbed instersections at gore points to be smooth, erring toward the side of more realistic, driveable lines.

Inventory Direction

Most routes are inventoried from West to East or North to South.  However, interstates are inventoried from South to North.  This is per FHWA guidelines and reflects that mile markers on interstates begin at the most southerly point or westerly point of an interstate.

Loops are inventoried in a clockwise direction beginning at the 6 o'clock position, or at the nearest intersection with the highest route classification to the 6 o'clock position.

All roadbeds are inventoried (i.e. digitized and measured) in the direction of the KG (Figure 1), with the exception of ancillary roadbeds such as ramps, connectors, grade separated connectors, and turn arounds, which are inventoried in the direction of traffic.

Divided Versus Undivided

All off-system roads are represented by a single roadbed (i.e. KG).  Off-system divided roads, such as boulevards, are represented in the linework as a single KG roadbed and are attributed in GRID as a boulevard in the Roadway Design asset.  On-system divided roads get additional roadbeds.

Medians

  • On-system roads become divided when a physical barrier (GRID Asset - Median) exists between the roadbeds.

  • If a median ends, the roadbeds should taper to the KG.

  • (Excerpt from the Median wiki page)... A roadbed is only considered to have a median if the total length of the median is longer than .1 miles (1/10 of a mile) before a major intersection or other significant break. [The beginning or end of a divided route without a median (like at an intersection) should have the roadbeds extend to the terminus of the route. This avoids having short KG segemnts with no roadbeds on an otherwise divided route.]  . A median with small breaks for left turns onto small side streets that don't have some sort of intersection control (and typically don't break the length of the median by more than 100 feet or so) is considered to be 'continuous'.

At-Grade Versus Grade-Separated Intersections/Interchanges

An intersection is the junction of two or more routes.  Junctions where cross traffic must stop at a sign or light are known as at-grade intersections.  Junctions that use grade separation are known as grade-separated intersections.

Self-Intersecting Routes

Self-intersecting line segments cannot be stored properly in GRID due to a bug, or limitation, with the Oracle Database that is the foundation for GRID.  As such, these route segments must not be snapped to themselves.  They must have a gap of more than 5 feet, or 0.001 miles, which is the lowest tolerance that the database will recognise as not being the same location. Otherwise, these segments will be dropped from the database and all assets will be lost.

Route Gaps, Zero-Length-Gaps, and Concurrencies

  • Measure gaps as the crow flies, except if its a (on-system) concurrency.
  • When a route has two segments seperated by another route segment that it is concurrent with, measure the length of the concurrent route segment in between,  add it to the end DFO value of the first segment, then use that as the begin DFO of the second segment.

Tolls

Fully-tolled facilities are digitized the same as on-system highways.  However, where there are dedicated managed lanes (e.g. HOV, HOT, or express lanes) along an otherwise non-tolled facility, supplimental roadbeds should be digitized to capture them, regardless of whether or not the roadbed is physically divided.  The only exception is in the rare situation where a non-toll normal mainlane is used part time as a toll or HOV/HOT lane. 

  • Code managed lane roadbeds as either TG or PG according to the mainlane roadbed they are next to.

  • Physical barriers need not be present (i.e. simple striping is enough).

  • Use the right supplimental, supplimental mainlane (TG) for cases where only a single, reversible managed lane is present.

One-Way Pairs

Roadbeds that are separated by a median, typically with buildings in it.  A way to differentiate a one-way Pair from a boulevard is the median is used for some purpose other than just 'green space'.  A classic example is a county courthouse in a town square with one way roadbeds surrounding it.


 

 Loops

Loops are inventoried in a clockwise direction beginning at the 6 o'clock position, or at nearest intersection to the 6 o'clock position with the highest route classification.

Lollipops

Lollipop routes are one of the self-intersecting features in our roadway inventory database.  They typically have a median that a road turns around.  If the median is small, digitize the centerline of the median.  However, if the median is a large area and thus >1 mile of centerline mileage would be missed by ditizing the centerline of the median, then draw around the middle area, and avoid self-intersecting the route.  

 


 

Cul-De-Sacs

Cul-de-sac is a French word, meaning literally the bottom (cul) of a bag (sac).  This design has been used for controlling traffic in residential areas, which allows motorists and cyclists to turn around to exit the area.  The centerline should go to the center of the cul-de-sac.

 

 

Roadbed Connections at the Ends of On-System Routes

The beginning or end of on-system routes should be connected by mainlanes (i.e. KG, LG, RG), rather than ancillary roadbed types (i.e. RP, GS, CN). Not doing so creates connectivity issues where routes meet (i.e. where one or more routes begin or end at another) when querying the network to only show centerlines and/or mainlanes.  This is problematic cartographically, but also has implications for other areas, such as pavement collection (e.g. where an LG or RG just seem to randomly end before the end of the route, and before a connection is made with the other route.

Intersections

Below are a set of common at-grade intersection types (as opposed to grade-separated interchanges).  These capture a variety of scenarios commonly encountered.  Nevertheless, there are always exceptional scenarios that need further exploration.  In those cases, bring the edit to a Mapping Branch meeting to discuss, and potentially document here.

Following are some general guidelines for digitizing intersections:

  • Digitize what is actually there, as much as this is possible within the guidelines established on this page.

  • Extend intersecting routes to all roadbeds physically possible to drive to.

  • Do not use a separate "turnaround"-type roadbed for the segment between the divided roadbeds.

  • When two routes meet at an intersection, extend one or the other through the intersection (i.e. across all roadbeds), rather than digitizing each to the centerline (KG).  See Route Hierachy below.  This practice ensures that higher class routes get the most mileage, while also facilitating the eventual use of the network for routing (otherwise, for example, imagine your car GPS as you proceed through an intersection...."take a left on FM9978 and proceed five thousandths of a mile to US18").

Route Hierarchy

  • On-system routes always take precedence over off-system routes.  Thus, where an on-system route meets an off-system route at an intersection, extend the on-system route through the intersection.

  • Where two on-system routes meet at a divided route, extend the higher class route accross all roadbeds.

  • Where two off-system routes meet, pick one and extend it across (preference to the lower numbered route).

NOTE: There may be specific circumstances (e.g. more complex intersections, or where the route's designation describes a specific start or end point) where one route takes precedence.

Single Roadbed Intesection with a Divided Highway

  • Extend to the fartherest roadbed physically possible

 

Single Roadbed Intersection with a Physically Divided Highway

  • Digitize the roadbeds in order to create only thosee connections that are physically possible to drive.
  • Do not extend the intersecting single roadbed to the KG (Note: when the newtwork is queried to only show KGs, this will present as a small gap in connectivity; however, at the scales where you generally do this for cartographic purposes, this gap will not be noticeable.  Plus, a realistic "network" is a more important factor)


Divided Highway Intersection with Another Divided Highway

  • Digitize the roadbeds in order to create all the connections that are physically possible to drive, then extend the KG to the roadbed perpendicular to the ends of the roadbeds. Special Considerations for Off-System Routes

Off-system roads each have a few special considerations or exceptions to practices used for on-system routes.

    • For off-system route name changes. An off-system road should have only one name along its entire length.
      • If the name of an entire, single record, use the street definition asset to update the name.  
      • If a route is to be divided into multiple names (e.g. North Pecan Road, and South Pecan Road), split the segment where the name changes and re-ETL (i.e. there should only be one name for a county road segment, or GID)
    • Off-system roads can have multiple segments, for example, when a long road is split into multiple segments with gaps between.  Measure the distance between segments as the crow flies and add this length to the end DFO value of the previous segment to obtain the begin DFO value of the next segment.
